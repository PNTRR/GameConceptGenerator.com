CCDI

Creative Crisis in Developed Industries

Why fight windmills? Because they are not windmills.

This is not just another idea generator website. This is an intellectual tool for a systemic problem. To understand how the solution works, one must first grasp the scale of the problem it was created for. Its name is Creative Crisis in Developed Industries (CCDI).


CCDI is a diagnosis we assign not to an individual, but to an entire industry that has reached peak commercial maturity. It's not your personal creative block. It is a systemic blockage of creativity. When an industry grows to hundreds of billions in revenue, its internal logic changes: from an artist's workshop, it transforms into a factory for predictable products. And it is at this peak that a paradox arises: the more resources, the fewer bold ideas.


This generator is a direct, practical response to the crisis described in our [full manifesto]. It exists not because "we were bored," but because CCDI is a real wall that everyone runs into when trying to create something new in the modern games industry.

The Three Faces of the Crisis: CCDI Symptoms

CCDI is not an abstraction. It has clear, observable symptoms that form a vicious cycle of stagnation. You encounter them every day, you just didn't know what to call it.


  • The Innovation Paradox.
    One would think that powerful engines and tools should usher in a golden age of game art. But the opposite happens: all that power is spent endlessly polishing outdated mechanics. Graphics become photorealistic, while gameplay remains stuck in the past decade. Technology becomes an end in itself, not a means for new experiences. We are not creating the games of the future, but holograms of past games in 4K resolution.
  • The Dominance of Successful Templates.
    In an industry where stakes reach hundreds of millions, past success is the only currency of trust. Any successful game is instantly canonized as a "proven working formula." Then this formula is replicated until the genre is exhausted. The problem is not with genres, but that any deviation from the template is perceived by publishers and investors as an unjustified risk. Thus, the industry stops creating games and starts stamping out product lines.
  • Systemic Devaluation of Risk.
    With such budgets, decisions stop being made by game designers. Power shifts to "product committees" and financial models in Excel. These models, by definition, filter out any radically new ideas—since their success cannot be guaranteed by extrapolating past data. Risking hundreds of millions on the unknown has become taboo. True experimentation survives only on the fringes—in the indie sector, where the stakes are not as high, and therefore, resources for implementation are minimal.

You are not an outside observer of this system. You are a part of it. Whether you are an independent developer who unconsciously copies the structure of an AAA hit to get noticed, or an employee of a major studio whose bold idea was shot down instantly "because the metrics don't align"—you already feel the pressure of CCDI.


This pressure creates a vicious cycle: publishers fund only templates → studios produce only templates → the audience becomes accustomed to templates and demands more of them → new developers look at the market and see that only templates are in demand. The circle closes. Originality becomes a systemic anomaly.

Why is this specific approach important?

Many see the symptoms: "games have become the same." But they treat the effect, not the cause, creating yet another generator of random fantasy worlds or genre combinations. Our approach is fundamentally different.


We strike not at the symptoms, but at the systemic mechanisms that generate them.


  1. Against Template Dominance: We are not offering a new template. We are offering a methodology for destroying any template through forced hybridization of their components. The algorithm does not know what a "successful formula" is—and that is its strength.
  2. Against the Innovation Paradox: We use technology not for polishing, but for generating creative raw material. We redirect the focus from the question "how to make it prettier?" to the question "what if we combine the incompatible?"
  3. Against Risk Devaluation: We have created an environment with zero cost of risk. Every generated concept is a permitted, even encouraged experiment. It is a training ground for the boldness that the system has squeezed out of you.

This is not an idea generator. This is a simulator for exiting the CCDI system. It does not give you a fish (a ready-made idea). It teaches you to fish in waters that the industry itself declared barren, showing that the fish are there—you just need to change your tackle and perspective.

The crisis of ideas is a crisis of courage and methodology. CCDI is a diagnosis we make together. This site is the prescription. Action is the treatment.
